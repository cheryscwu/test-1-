Location & Environmental Context

Saibai Island is located in the north-western Torres Strait, about 4 km south of Papua New Guinea. The island faces ongoing water challenges intensified by climate change, including saltwater intrusion, tidal flooding, and extended dry periods that reduce freshwater availability.

The islandâ€™s main water sources are the central lagoon and household rainwater tanks. However, both often show high turbidity and contamination risks, making the water unsafe for drinking. Its remote location also increases transport costs and maintenance difficulties (EWB Challenge, 2025).

Community & Infrastructure Challenges

Saibaiâ€™s isolation creates major logistical and cost challenges for maintaining water infrastructure. These issues affect both the community and the Torres Strait Island Regional Council (TSIRC), which oversees water operations on the island.

Public Health Impact

Unsafe water quality and high turbidity increase the risk of waterborne diseases, impacting community health and wellbeing.

Energy Use

Because of poor water quality, residents boil water for disinfection. This method is safe but uses a lot of energy and increases household costs.

Trust & Reliability

Failure to meet the turbidity requirement below 1 NTU reduces confidence in TSIRCâ€™s water systems and management reliability.

Water Quality Concern

Current turbidity levels range between 1 and 2 NTU, exceeding the required standard of below 1 NTU. This issue endangers public health and reduces trust in local water management.

Technical Assessment

The existing filtration system, using five and one micron filters, cannot consistently meet the turbidity target. Chlorine dosing, rainwater harvesting, and small desalination units provide temporary relief but are difficult and expensive to maintain on Saibai Island.

Treating all water at the same level also wastes energy, since non-drinking water does not need the same purification as potable water.

The proposed solution introduces a dual water system that separates drinking and non-drinking water. This approach aims to:

  • Achieve consistent turbidity below 1 NTU for safe drinking water.
  • Reduce energy use and resource waste during treatment.
  • Improve long-term reliability and community confidence.
  • Provide a practical, affordable, and sustainable design suitable for Saibai Island.

These goals support a safer and more resilient water system for the Saibai Island community.
